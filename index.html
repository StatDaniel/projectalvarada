II. Introduction & Background

A climate-driven public-health crisis

The Federative Republic of Brazil, home to 15–20% of the world's biodiversity and the vast Amazon basin, faces a critical public-health crisis driven by climate change and rapid urbanization. This crisis arises from dengue fever. Brazil presents optimal breeding conditions for the Aedes aegypti mosquito vector, due to its warm tropical climate averaging around 25.5 °C.

As a country in Stage 4 of the Demographic Transition Model, with low death and birth rates, population stability relies heavily on maintaining and regulating public health. In 2025 there were 1,660,190 probable cases reported, alongside 1,762 confirmed deaths. Vulnerable communities are particularly targeted as it mainly impacts densely populated coastal areas. Due to poor infrastructure in the Southeast and Northeast regions, heavy rainfall leads to lots of standing water, generating a greater number of mosquitoes.

Demographic Transition — Stage 4

A low stationary phase

Brazil is at Stage 4 of the Demographic Transition Model — a 'low stationary phase' with low birth rates and low death rates, meaning population growth stabilises at near 0.

  • 2024 death rate: 7 deaths per 1,000 people

II & III. Core Problem · Transmission

Dengue and environmental factors

Brazil faces a major mosquito-borne disease problem, particularly from dengue — driven by warm climates, rainfall and urban conditions that allow mosquitoes to spread diseases affecting public health.

Why the coast, why the rainy season

Brazil has the greatest population density near the coast. Heavy rainfall — particularly during the wet season — leads to standing water build up, meaning outbreaks occur mainly during the rainy season. Even a small amount of standing water makes an optimal breeding ground for mosquitoes. Such water may be found in gutters, buckets, containers etc.

Southeast & Northeast

Mosquito-borne diseases mostly spread in the southeast and northeast regions of Brazil as they have optimal weather conditions (20–25°C) and rainy environments, and are densely populated. From 2001 to 2007, 64% of all dengue cases in South America were reported here, with Brazil holding the highest fatality rate.

Dengue's route to Brazil

Dengue likely originated in Africa or Southeast Asia. It spread through shipping routes during the 18–20th century. The main carrier is Aedes aegypti. Symptoms include fever, aches and pain, nausea and vomiting, bleeding, and belly pain and tenderness.

Vaccines against dengue have been decreasing recently. The reasons: limited awareness of the vaccine, people underestimating the disease, and the vaccine being restricted to certain age groups (generally 10–14 years) due to limited supply.

How the virus hijacks the cell

Transmission: a mosquito first bites an infected person; the virus enters and multiplies inside the mosquito; once it bites another person the virus is spread to a new human host.

Mosquito-borne viruses are made up of RNA (genetic instructions, not DNA), a protein shell and a fatty outer coat that attaches to human cells and releases its RNA, causing the human cell to be hijacked and copy the new virus RNA to make more virus cells.

Dengue

Causes fever, body pain. Originated in Africa or Southeast Asia; spread via shipping routes during 18–20th century. Main carrier: Aedes aegypti.

Zika

First discovered in Uganda in 1947 in the Zika Forest. Dangerous for newborns. Small African/Asian outbreaks for decades before a large outbreak in Brazil caused birth defects.

Chikungunya

First discovered in Tanzania in 1952. Causes joint pain. Spread across Africa, Asia and Indian Ocean islands, reaching the Americas and Brazil in the 2010s.

Yellow Fever

Introduced to the Americas in the 17th century via the transatlantic slave trade. Large epidemics in coastal cities such as Rio de Janeiro. Killed thousands of European sailors and immigrants. Causes liver damage, bleeding problems and organ failure in severe cases.

Malaria

Early spread from colonisation and control by European colonisers; introduced via the African slave trade. Internal migration exposed immunity-compromised workers to highly malarious zones (Amazon, coastal plains). Railroad construction and industrial boom concentrated vulnerable people in remote, high-risk jungle environments. Causes red blood cell destruction, repeated fever cycles and organ blockage.

Notable Vectors

Anopheles cruzi — identified 1903 by Adolpho Lutz, breeding in bromeliads along mountainous railroad routes.
Anopheles darlingi — by early 1930s, primary malaria vector across river basins and coastal lowlands like the Baixada Fluminense.
Anopheles gambiae — invasive African species introduced to Natal in 1930 via French mail planes/ships from Senegal; triggered devastating epidemics in northeastern Brazil by 1938.

IV. Strategic Justification

Why dengue, not yellow fever

We initially debated whether to investigate another mosquito-borne disease such as yellow fever, but we decided to focus on dengue — a problem tied to climate change, urban infrastructure and managing large-scale outbreaks, rather than one focused on immunization policy, global vaccine distribution and high-fatality intervention.

V. Socioeconomic Impact

The cost beyond the disease

Dengue is a major problem for Brazil not solely due to the disease, but the socioeconomic impact on society. It overwhelms healthcare systems (particularly during rainy season), deepens social inequality (impacting low-income households harder due to poor urban infrastructure), harms the economy (national productivity falls when many people fall ill at once), and leads to vaccine supply bottlenecking. We cannot rely on quick immunization — we must fix the root cause and structural issues.

III. Past International Action

What has been tried before

Biological control

Fish have been used to eliminate mosquitoes in containers.

Chemical control

Insecticides have been used in the past against the main strain of mosquitoes spreading the disease — however some populations of insecticide-resistant mosquitoes have also been discovered.

Trapping

Lethal ovitraps have been tested.

Vaccination

A vaccine has been developed, but is limited to children of 10 to 14 years old from public health, since vaccine supplies are limited.

Wolbachia

Releasing Aedes aegypti mosquitoes infected with Wolbachia, which reduces their lifespan by 50% and makes it harder for the dengue virus to develop. Wolbachia is the best option — releasing genetically modified mosquitoes would be too expensive, without permanent effect and with the chance to fail completely. Wolbachia already spreads naturally, whereas GM approaches would need continuous releases and do not solve the root problem.

Project Alvorada — Proposed Solution

Wolbachia biology + resilient infrastructure

Mosquitoes are becoming ever more resistant to insecticides, and it is simply unfeasible to rebuild entire cities overnight. Brazil believes the most effective solution is to deploy Wolbachia-infected mosquitoes for immediate biological protection, while simultaneously updating local infrastructure to permanently prevent mosquito reproduction.

I. Wolbachia

We would deploy Wolbachia-infected mosquitoes during targeted high seasons. Wolbachia shortens the vector's lifespan by up to 50% and hinders the mosquito's ability to reproduce the dengue virus. Wolbachia is a naturally occurring bacterium found in Aedes aegypti.

During high seasons in high-burden urban hubs, targeted high-volume releases will take place. Because the bacterium is passed down from female mosquitoes to their offspring, the Wolbachia trait becomes self-sustaining in the environment over time.

Capital Expenditure — one-time

Before we release any mosquitoes, we need to breed them first in a high-technology 'bio-factory'.

  • Output: A large, automated factory (e.g. Fiocruz in Brazil)
≈ $19 million

Operational Expenditure — day-to-day

In order for the project to run smoothly we need continuous ongoing expenses to keep the project running in high seasons.

  • Climate control, electricity for lab
  • Large quantities of mosquito food
  • Salaries for scientists
  • Gas for vehicles
≈ $11 million

Economic & Cost-Effectiveness

Return on investment: for every $1 the government invests in the Wolbachia program, they save an estimated $43 to $549 in the long run.

  • Dengue outbreaks can cost $500M – $1.6B annually in medicine, hospitalizations and lost work hours
  • As Wolbachia is a self-sustaining trait passed down, OpEx eventually drops to 0 while the healthcare system saves millions of dollars every year
$1 → $43–$549

II. Infrastructure

  • We would improve drainage systems. Better drainage allows us to control rain and floodwater better, reducing pools of still water — perfect environments for mosquitoes to reproduce.
  • Storage of water would be made safer, with better covers to prevent mosquitoes from laying eggs.
  • Buildings would use more screens, sealed water tanks and ventilation systems to prevent mosquitoes from getting in, with regular checks for gutters and drains.

Capital Expenditure — one-time

We need to invest in updated infrastructure across multiple areas and regions.

  • Upgrade to continuous running water
  • Smart water metering
$23 billion / 10 yrs

Operational Expenditure — day-to-day

We need continuous monitoring and construction of the buildings over the years.

  • Engineering drainage networks
  • Closed drainage
$3.5 billion / year

Economic & Cost-Effectiveness

Fewer dengue cases, hospital costs, deaths and healthcare costs — fewer sick days and better communities.

  • Estimated savings from reduced costs across 10 years
≈ $50 billion
